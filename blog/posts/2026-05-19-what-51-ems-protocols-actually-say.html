I Compared 51 EMS Protocols From Across the Country. Here's What I Noticed.

By Jaden • May 19, 2026

I run an app that EMTs and medics use to study their own service's protocols. That means I have access to hundreds of real EMS protocol documents from all over the country.

So I compared a bunch of them. Here's what I found in 51.

If you've worked at more than one EMS agency, you already know the secret: the basics transfer, the details don't.

You'll show up to a new service knowing how to run a cardiac arrest, how to set up a 12-lead, how to push epi, how to start an IO. None of that will surprise you. The surprises will be small and dangerous:

  • The midazolam dose is different.
  • The drug for a violent patient is different.
  • The thing you used to do on your own now needs a call to medical control.
  • The vial you keep reaching for isn't on the truck.

I wanted to know how much of this is just shop talk and how much is real. So I actually looked.

I run ProtoQuiz, an app where EMTs and paramedics upload their service's protocol PDF and quiz themselves on it. Because of that, I've ended up with hundreds of real EMS protocols from all over the country, with the drugs pulled out and lined up in a way I can actually compare.

I picked the 51 cleanest ones — spread across every region of the US, a mix of statewide, county, big-city, and air-medical services — and went through them.

Three patterns jumped out. Here they are, then what I think they mean for anyone working a truck.

1. Almost every service carries the same 19 drugs

When I lined all 51 up, 19 drugs showed up in at least 4 out of every 5 services. I'll call these the common core — the drugs you can basically count on being on the truck no matter where in the country you're working:

Naloxone, Albuterol, Atropine, Epinephrine, Midazolam, Nitroglycerin, Sodium Bicarbonate, Diphenhydramine, Fentanyl, Aspirin, Lidocaine, Amiodarone, Glucagon, Magnesium Sulfate, Adenosine, Ketamine, Oral Glucose, Tranexamic Acid (TXA), Acetaminophen.

A couple of things stood out to me.

TXA and ketamine are part of that core now. A decade ago, neither would have made a top-20 list. Here, TXA showed up in 82% of services and ketamine in 86%. Ketamine is the Swiss Army knife of the box — sedation, pain, agitation, induction for intubation, all from one vial. That versatility is probably why it spread so fast.

Services agree more on the job than on the drug. Ask "does this service carry fentanyl?" and 94% say yes. Ask "does this service carry something for moderate pain" (fentanyl, morphine, or hydromorphone) and it's 96%. Almost everyone has a tool for the job — they just don't always pick the same one.

This common core is most of any service's drug box. On average, those 19 drugs make up about 45% of a given service's whole formulary. For busy street-ALS services — Denver Metro, Boulder County, Eastside WA, NJ, Massachusetts — they make up 55% to 67% of the list.

So when someone says "I know my drugs," it's true — they already know roughly two-thirds of any new service's drug box the day they walk in.

It's the other third that gets people.

2. Even the shared drugs aren't dosed the same

You already know epi does a dozen different jobs, and each one has its own dose — the 1 mg arrest dose, a push-dose pressor for shock, a drip for the post-arrest patient. So I held all of that still and looked at one exact thing: the initial adult IM dose for anaphylaxis. Same drug, same indication, same point in the algorithm — and it still doesn't agree from service to service:

ServiceInitial adult IM epi, anaphylaxis
Alameda County (ADAP, CA)0.15 mg IM
Boulder County0.3 mg (1:1,000) IM
Antelope Valley CPG (CA)0.5 mg IM
Denver Metro EMSno fixed adult number; repeat q20min IM

Same first dose, same patient — and it's written as 0.15, 0.3, and 0.5 mg depending on where you work. (Yes, every one of these has its own IV-push and drip orders for the refractory case too — but those are a separate step, so I'm not mixing them in here.)

That matters more than it sounds. If you trained somewhere that did 0.3 mg IM and move to a place that does 0.5, your reflex dose is now low. Move the other way and you're giving more than double what the new protocol calls for. Same drug, same patient, same call — different number.

Midazolam for a seizure is even messier. Some services start at 10 mg IM, some at 5, some at 2 and titrate up. For the exact same emergency, the first IM dose ranges from 2 mg to 10 mg depending on where you work — and a 2 mg start may not stop a seizure that a 10 mg start would.

This is the part that doesn't transfer between services. Even the routine drugs have a catch.

3. The big algorithms match. The fine print doesn't.

Quick note on what I did here, because it's different from the drug part. The drug counts and dose tables above come from all 51 protocols. Algorithms are a different kind of work — you have to actually read the document, not just pull a drug list — so for this part I sat down with a smaller set: 12 services, four of them line by line (Denver Metro, DC Fire, North Carolina, Aurora South WI) and eight more at the structural level. I'm not going to pretend I read every page of all 51; that's a different project. But across the 12, nine major algorithms — cardiac arrest, anaphylaxis, STEMI, sepsis, stroke, trauma triage, OB, behavioral, and when to call a code in the field — were enough to see the pattern clearly.

Eight of the nine are basically the same everywhere. The overall flow of how EMS handles a cardiac arrest, or an anaphylaxis, or a STEMI is nearly identical from service to service. The national standards (ACLS, AHA, the CDC trauma criteria) won — everybody runs the same playbook.

The differences are in the fine print:

  1. The numbers. What counts as a STEMI (1.0 mm of ST elevation at some services, 2.0 at others), how long before you can call a code (20 vs 30 minutes), how much fluid in sepsis, what blood pressure to allow in trauma.
  2. Which drug at each step. Which medication is the go-to, which is backup, which is held in reserve.
  3. Whether you need a doctor's OK. Some services let a medic call a code in the field on their own; others make you phone medical control for the exact same situation. Same criteria, different permission.
  4. Where "pediatric" starts. The age cutoff for "kid" changes from protocol to protocol — sometimes even within the same service, depending on the decision.

The one that really splits is RSI (using drugs to help intubate). Of the 12 I looked at closely, 4 don't allow it at all (North Carolina, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Northwest Chicago).

And where it is allowed, the details are all different. Some prefer one paralytic, others another. Some put ketamine first for sedation afterward, others fentanyl. Some let a medic do it on standing orders; others require sign-off even if your cert allows it. One procedure, several different rulebooks.

The protocol for a violently agitated patient was the clearest example — three genuinely different philosophies:

  • Pick from a menu (Denver Metro): score the patient, then choose midazolam or an antipsychotic.
  • Give the combo (DC Fire): haloperidol + midazolam together.
  • Ketamine first (Aurora South WI): skip the scoring, ketamine is the primary drug.

Those aren't dosing nitpicks — they're three different beliefs about how to handle the same patient, on a topic EMS as a field is still arguing about. If you train people across services, you have to teach the thinking, not just the number.

What's actually dangerous when you switch services

I figured the risky part of changing agencies would be the new stuff you've never used. It's actually the opposite.

The risky part is what your old service had that the new one doesn't.

Look at two real services 30 miles apart — Denver Metro and Boulder County, both Colorado, both current.

  • They share 41 drugs.
  • Boulder carries 2 that Denver doesn't (a pair of eye-numbing drops).
  • Denver carries 16 that Boulder doesn't. Most are minor, but a few aren't: calcium, dopamine, pralidoxime, blood, diltiazem.

A medic moving from Denver to Boulder loses the calcium they'd reach for in a hyperkalemic arrest. Loses dopamine for cardiogenic shock. Loses pralidoxime for a pesticide exposure. Loses blood for major bleeding — now it's TXA and fluids only.

Their hand keeps going for a vial that isn't on the truck anymore. That's the real hazard.

The brand-new drugs at a service get all the attention. It's the ones quietly missing that bite you.

Why a national EMS protocol is harder than it sounds

There actually is a national one — the National Model EMS Clinical Guidelines. It's in my set. It's a perfectly reasonable protocol. So why doesn't everyone just use it?

After going through 51 of these, the reasons are pretty clear:

  1. States don't agree on what each cert level can do. Wisconsin has a tier that's allowed to do things that are paramedic-only elsewhere. A single national protocol would either hold everyone to the lowest level or have to spell out scope state by state.
  2. A lot of the choices are preference, not science. Which steroid, which pressor, which sedative — the evidence is fine for several options, so each service picks based on its own medical director and pharmacy. There's no obviously-correct national answer to lock in.
  3. Who's allowed to pull the trigger varies. Whether a medic can act on their own or has to call a doctor first is a local decision. A national rule saying "you can always do X" would step on that.
  4. Regions just practice differently. The Northeast leans on certain sedatives and pressors, the South on certain anti-nausea drugs, the Midwest carries things the coasts don't. Some of it's supply, some of it's the local teaching hospitals, some of it's just how it's always been done. A single document won't erase that.
  5. Different kinds of services need different things. Maryland tracks community-paramedic drugs, air services carry blood, fire services carry HazMat antidotes. A national protocol would have to be a set of modules you mix and match — not one document.

The part everyone already agrees on — the common core of drugs and the big algorithms — is basically standardized today. The useful thing isn't a single "one true protocol." It's a clear way to show how your service differs from the next one.

That's what I'm building next.

What I'm doing with this data

If you're an EMT or a medic and you've ever switched services, you already know this is the right shape. And if you're about to switch for the first time — your hand is going to reach for vials out of habit. The whole job is knowing which ones are still there.

How I did this — method & limitations

How I did it

Two layers, and they cover different amounts of the set:

  • Drugs (all 51 protocols): 2,394 drug entries pulled from every one, cleaned down to 258 distinct drugs. The common core, the dose tables, and the per-service comparisons all come from the full 51.
  • Algorithms (12 of the 51): 4 services read line by line (Denver Metro, DC Fire, North Carolina, Aurora South WI) + 8 more reviewed structurally. 9 algorithms mapped service by service. I did not read every page of all 51 for this part.
  • 4 regions: West 22, South 9, Northeast 9, Midwest 6 (plus 1 national, 1 international, 3 where I couldn't pin the state)
  • Pulled May 2026

What this isn't

  • I didn't interview anyone — this is reading the documents, not surveying medics
  • I'm not saying any service is right or wrong, only that they differ
  • It's US-focused (the one New Zealand set is just a curiosity)
  • I'm not proposing a national protocol

Worth being honest about

  • 51 is a small slice of the thousands of EMS systems out there
  • The extraction isn't perfect — about 95 dose entries got flagged for review
  • The algorithm findings lean on 12 services, not all 51 — so treat those as a strong read of the pattern, not a full census
  • Only 3 air-medical services, which is too few to generalize about flight crews
