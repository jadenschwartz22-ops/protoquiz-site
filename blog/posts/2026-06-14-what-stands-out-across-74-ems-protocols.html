What Stands Out Across 53 EMS Protocols (Uploaded by the Providers Who Study Them)

By Jaden • June 14, 2026 • 17 min read

Nobody scraped these protocols. Every one of the 53 documents in this survey was uploaded by a real EMS provider — an EMT, a paramedic, a flight clinician — who wanted to study their own agency's rules inside the ProtoQuiz app. The dataset is a byproduct of working clinicians cramming for their own jobs. That makes it a strange and honest cross-section of American prehospital medicine: not the protocols a researcher would pick, but the protocols people actually carry and worry about getting wrong.

Taken together, those uploads span 53 complete protocol documents across 30 distinct states and regions, including the NASEMSO national model, St John New Zealand, and Alberta. This survey reads across all of them and pulls out what differs — medications, algorithms and decision trees, procedures and skills, scope of practice, system policies, and outright outliers. It is a broad survey, not a deep dive: every item is tied to the source document and page it came from, and every item survived a three-skeptic, two-of-three verification pass (see the methods note at the end). Several real-world systems publish across multiple documents, so the document count exceeds the count of distinct systems.

Five findings that surprised me

Maple syrup is an approved oral glucose. Vermont lists maple syrup as an acceptable source of oral glucose for the conscious hypoglycemic patient. Vermont EMS Statewide Protocols

Naloxone is banned in cardiac arrest — in red text. San Diego County explicitly prohibits naloxone during arrest, contradicting the common "it won't hurt" practice. San Diego County EMS, p.57

A horizontal-gaze stroke test instead of FAST. Alabama uses a proprietary EMSA scale (Eyes / Motor / Speech) where "Eyes" is a gaze-tracking test, not facial droop. Alabama Statewide EMS Protocols, p.206

Field thrombolytics for STEMI. St John New Zealand gives tenecteplase plus heparin 5000 U on a standing order — none of the US systems do this. St John New Zealand, p.62

Field perimortem cesarean section. Mayo Clinic Ambulance authorizes it at 4 minutes without ROSC, ≥20 weeks gestation — OR-level surgery on the side of the road. Mayo Clinic Ambulance, p.255

Medications

Medications are the biggest of the three categories — 5,556 entries in all. Formulary size ranges from just 4 medications at Davidson County EMS (NC) up to 177 medications at the Kentucky Board of EMS, a 44-fold spread. The most notable medication standouts:

  • Adenosine first dose skips 6 mg. Most systems start at 6 mg then 12 mg; a cluster gives 12 mg as dose #1 — Boulder County (p.132), Denver Metro (p.123), San Antonio/STRAC (p.140), West Palm Beach (p.137), and Thompson Valley (p.387).
  • TXA single dose splits 1 g vs 2 g. Greater Miami Valley, NY State Collaborative, San Antonio/STRAC, UT LIFESTAR, Mountain West, Kershaw County, and WV-2025 use 2 g; PA, MA, the NASEMSO model, Boulder, and Berrien use 1 g.
  • Nebulized TXA for bleeding. Greater Miami Valley (p.271, pediatric post-tonsillectomy p.317) and Mayo Clinic Ambulance nebulize TXA 250–500 mg for oropharyngeal hemorrhage; Salem Health nebulizes 500 mg for hemoptysis (p.103). Most systems carry TXA IV-only.
  • Conservative naloxone starting doses. Boulder County and Denver Metro start at 0.5 mg titrated; UT LIFESTAR starts at 0.05–0.4 mg titrated to respiratory effort — against the 2–4 mg IN bolus standard (Berrien, Michigan, NY, Mayo, Greater Miami).
  • Nebulized opioids. Boulder County allows fentanyl 1–2 mcg/kg and morphine 0.1 mg/kg (max 5 mg) by nebulized route for pain (p.170) — essentially unique in the corpus.
  • Mayo Clinic Ambulance carries an ICU-grade transfer formulary including inhaled nitric oxide, oxytocin, insulin, nicardipine, nitroprusside, piperacillin-tazobactam, and a lidocaine 5% patch — far beyond standard 911 stock.
  • St John New Zealand uses metaraminol (0.5–1 mg IV / 2 mg/hr infusion, p.508) as its primary push/infusion pressor for hypotension — a vasopressor stocked by no US system in the set — and carries methoxyflurane (Penthrox) inhalers plus loratadine instead of diphenhydramine.
  • Half-dose cardiac-arrest epinephrine. San Antonio/Bexar-Frio uses 0.5 mg IV/IO q5min in its arrest algorithms (p.110), not the AHA-standard 1 mg.
  • Field-initiated buprenorphine for opioid withdrawal appears in multiple systems: authorized at all cert levels (EMT and up) in Massachusetts (p.109), a 16 mg SL film in the San Antonio/Bexar-Frio formulary, and a formal "CORe" program with COWS scoring at West Palm Beach.
  • Relic drugs in Kentucky. The 2021 Kentucky document still lists streptokinase alongside tPA, diazoxide 5 mg/kg IV push for hypertensive crisis, PASG/MAST trousers, digital intubation, and blind nasotracheal intubation (p.331).
  • Maple syrup as oral glucose. Vermont's statewide protocols name maple syrup as an acceptable source of oral glucose for the conscious hypoglycemic patient — a regional touch found nowhere else in the corpus.
  • Dextrose 10% replaces D50 outright. Santa Cruz County uses D10 for all hypoglycemia across adults, peds, and neonates — no D50 or D25 anywhere in the formulary (adult 25 g / 250 mL IV; peds 5 mL/kg, p.228). LA County likewise uses D10 125 mL instead of D50, citing vascular irritation and rebound hyperglycemia from concentrated dextrose (p.25).
  • St John New Zealand uses metaraminol (0.5–1 mg IV / 2 mg/hr infusion, p.508) as its primary push/infusion pressor — a vasopressor stocked by no US system in the set — and carries methoxyflurane (Penthrox) inhalers plus loratadine instead of diphenhydramine. (Listed above; repeated for emphasis on pressor choice.)
  • Clevidipine on a ground statewide model. Alabama carries clevidipine (Cleviprex), an IV calcium-channel-blocker lipid emulsion, at Critical Care level for hypertensive emergency with ICU-grade titration — start 1–2 mg/hr, double every 90 seconds, max 32 mg/hr (p.112). Rare outside the ICU.
  • Agency-level heparin authorization. Alabama double-gates heparin: it requires Critical Care certification and the agency itself must be specifically authorized to carry it (80 units/kg bolus, max 4,000–7,500 units; p.129). Authorization beyond the individual cert level is unusual.
  • Push-dose epi as a county standing order. San Diego County standardizes push-dose epinephrine 1:100,000 across at least six protocols (shock, sepsis, anaphylaxis, ROSC, bradycardia, peds shock) with one printed mixing recipe and no OLMC required (p.77); Santa Cruz embeds it the same way across anaphylaxis, sepsis, shock, and post-ROSC (p.295).
  • Prehospital buprenorphine MAT in San Diego. Paramedics score the 11-item COWS scale and, for COWS ≥8 in patients ≥16, may give buprenorphine-naloxone 16 mg/4 mg SL titrated to 24 mg/6 mg, paired with a Leave-Behind Naloxone program (p.85).
  • Curated activated-charcoal list. San Diego authorizes activated charcoal 50 g PO only within 60 minutes, gag reflex intact, for a specific 10-substance list (acetaminophen, colchicine, beta-blockers, CCBs, salicylates, valproate, oral anticoagulants, paraquat, Amanita mushrooms, or Poison Control direction) rather than a blanket "significant ingestion" rule (p.70).
  • Capped cardiac-arrest epinephrine. LA County hard-caps arrest epi at 3 mg total (1 mg q3–5 min), diverging from the AHA's no-ceiling repeat dosing (p.42).
  • Hard-ceiling ketamine analgesia. San Diego restricts ketamine analgesia to ≥15 yr with a fixed, non-weight-adjusted ceiling of 30 mg IV / 50 mg IN per dose, explicitly for sub-dissociative analgesia only and preferred over opioids when SBP <100 (p.81).

Anaphylaxis IM epinephrine is the most consistent finding across all 53 (mostly 0.3 mg), but St John NZ, Kershaw County, and San Antonio/Bexar-Frio use 0.5 mg IM.

Algorithms & decision trees

These protocols lean heavily on decision trees — 2,470 of them in all, topping out at 85 at the New York State Collaborative. The same emergency is rarely handled the same way twice. The standouts below are grouped by presentation; every item is page-cited.

Cardiac arrest

  • Antiarrhythmic is a regional preference, not a constant. Berrien County (MI) explicitly selected lidocaine (1 mg/kg, max 3 mg/kg) for all cardiac arrest and flags amiodarone 300 mg as "not selected"; most systems list amiodarone first-line, and several (PA, Boulder, Alameda) treat the two as co-equal medical-director options (p.91).
  • Epinephrine dosing diverges sharply. Bexar/Frio (TX) gives Epi 0.5 mg IV every 5 min in asystole/PEA (half the standard 1 mg) or a 1 mcg/kg/min infusion; Boulder County caps epinephrine at 3 standing-order doses; most systems give 1 mg q3-5 min with no ceiling (p.110).
  • Oklahoma gates repeat epi on age and vitals. A paramedic must call medical control before any additional epi if the patient is ≥50, has cardiac history, or BP >140/90 — everywhere else repeat epi is a standing order (p.209).
  • Vasopressin survives despite 2020 AHA removing it. Kentucky allows Vasopressin 40 U to replace the first or second epi dose; Greater Miami/Miami carry it as a "just-in-time" standing order; NY State, Alameda and Utah explicitly note "no vasopressin" (p.54).
  • Traumatic arrest, opposite philosophies. TVEMS (TN) explicitly does not support epinephrine in traumatic arrest (reversible-cause-focused, rapid transport); Alameda, PA and AMR still give epi 1 mg while addressing the same causes (p.401).
  • ECMO/ECPR is spreading. Bexar/Frio (TX) bakes a prehospital ECMO/ECPR pathway into its adult arrest algorithm ("ECMO alert if <30 min to a Comprehensive Resuscitation Center", p.107). San Diego County goes further with a formal 5-criteria ECPR transport algorithm — age 18–70, witnessed arrest with CPR within 5 min, refractory VF/pVT after ≥2 defibs, mechanical CPR running, and arrest-to-ECPR-center arrival ≤45 min — mandating bypass of non-ECPR STEMI centers even if closer (p.61).
  • Naloxone banned in arrest. San Diego County explicitly states, in red text, that naloxone is not authorized in cardiac arrest — contradicting the widespread "won't hurt" empiric-naloxone habit (p.57).
  • Hypothermic-arrest restraint. San Diego limits epinephrine to a single dose and withholds all antiarrhythmics until core temperature ≥86°F / 30°C, noting defibrillation may be ineffective below 30°C — rewarming first (p.63).
  • SGA preferred over the tube. LA County explicitly prefers a supraglottic airway over endotracheal intubation in arrest (p.42), and Santa Cruz makes the King LT the preferred advanced airway with ETI relegated to backup, citing compression interruptions during intubation (p.279).

Field termination of resuscitation (TOR)

  • Alameda County (CA) caps shocks. One of its simultaneous TOR criteria is "≤2 defibrillations delivered" — deliver a third shock and the patient can no longer be field-terminated. It also applies the same TOR criteria to pediatric medical arrest, where most systems exclude under-18 entirely (p.92).
  • Minimum-effort floors vary. AMR/Air Care require 30 minutes of ACLS before a PEA field death; most California systems and Lake Cities use 20 minutes; Berrien splits it by provider level (p.16). Nearly all require a medical-control call for TOR — but TVEMS allows field pronouncement of a non-salvageable traumatic arrest without one.
  • The longest on-scene effort in the set. LA County gives refractory VF/pVT up to 40 minutes of on-scene ALS before TOR (non-shockable rhythms reach TOR at 20 min with no ROSC, p.45) — one of the most aggressive resuscitation floors in the corpus.
  • A six-criteria standing-order TOR. San Diego permits field termination on standing order only when all six conditions hold at once: persistent asystole, unwitnessed by bystanders and EMS, no bystander CPR, no AED shock delivered, no pulses at any time, and ≥20 min on scene. Miss any one and it routes to a physician (p.57).
  • EtCO2 as a stop signal. Santa Cruz encodes EtCO2 ≤10 mmHg sustained for 20 contiguous minutes after starting ACLS as a field criterion to discontinue resuscitation in PEA/asystole (p.305).

Seizure / status epilepticus

  • When you must call varies from dose 1 to dose 3. Riverside/REMSA requires a base-hospital call before any repeat benzodiazepine; other systems allow two or three standing-order doses first.
  • Second-line agent splits. Several systems escalate to ketamine after two benzo doses (Metro Regional 2 mg/kg IM as a standing order; WPB Fire 100 mg IV); Utah LifeStar runs the most aggressive ladder (lorazepam → levetiracetam 2 g → ketamine load + infusion) (p.50, p.60).
  • Retained rectal diazepam. Madison FD, Northwest Community, Ada County, NJ and Mass-peds keep PR diazepam 0.5 mg/kg for the no-IV pediatric branch (p.12).
  • Salem Health / Polk County (OR) embeds a full refusal-of-transport decision node inside the seizure algorithm: a known epileptic may be left on scene only if all 7 criteria are met (p.75).

Pain management

  • Pennsylvania inverts the usual gate. Opioids/ketamine are a standing order only for severe pain (>7/10); treating mild or moderate pain requires a medical-control call (p.139).
  • Pediatric opioids, adults-only autonomy. The 2018 state protocols make all pediatric opioid analgesia medical-control-only while the same drugs are standing orders for adults (p.236).
  • Geriatric dose-reduction age is inconsistent — West Virginia cuts morphine at ≥55 and fentanyl at ≥65; Greater Miami does not reduce until >69 (p.74). Aurora (WI) carries a standalone sickle-cell algorithm that makes hydromorphone the preferred first-line opioid (p.101).

Sepsis

  • Field antibiotics split three ways. Bexar/Frio (TX) makes Ceftriaxone 2 g a paramedic standing order (Cefepime second-line); many systems carry no field antibiotic at all (p.252).
  • Recognition criteria diverge between SIRS, qSOFA, and a mandatory capnography gate — NWC EMSS requires EtCO2 ≤31 plus qSOFA (p.41), and LA County encodes EtCO2 ≤25 mmHg as a diagnostic sepsis indicator alongside temp, HR >90, RR >20, AMS, and SBP <90 (p.27). Standing-order fluid ceilings range 3x: Miami caps crystalloid at 1 L before a call (p.111). TVEMS uses an epinephrine infusion (not norepinephrine) as its septic-shock pressor (p.405).

Stroke

  • No shared LVO screening tool. Nearly every agency picked a different scale and cutoff — LAMS/S-LAMS ≥4 (Riverside, NYC), FAST-ED ≥4 (Mass), C-STAT ≥2 (AMR), RACE >5 (Davidson) (p.250).
  • Thrombectomy-routing time window varies nearly 7x — New York routes to a stroke center only if last-known-well is ≤3.5 h; NYC REMAC allows up to 24 h (p.136).
  • Pennsylvania's stroke scale uses the phrase "The sky is blue in Pennsylvania" rather than the generic Cincinnati phrase (p.149). Kentucky and Thompson Valley (CO) carry an explicit line that nitroglycerin must not be used for stroke-associated hypertension (p.88).
  • Alabama abandons FAST entirely. The statewide protocols use a proprietary EMSA scale — Eyes / Motor / Speech-Aphasia — where "Eyes" is a horizontal-gaze tracking test rather than the standard facial-droop screen, forcing providers to learn an Alabama-specific assessment (p.206).
  • LA County's home-grown LVO triage. Stroke routing runs on the county's own mLAPSS plus LAMS tools: a LAMS of 4–5 with last-known-well <24 h routes to a Comprehensive Stroke Center if within 30 min, bypassing the nearest stroke center (p.89). San Diego uses BE-FAST plus FAST-ED for LVO triage.

Rhythm management & other

  • Adenosine first dose. Denver Metro skips the conventional 6 mg and starts SVT at 12 mg (p.40).
  • A-fib/VT agent forks. Berrien County (MI) selected verapamil for A-fib (not diltiazem) and lidocaine for stable VT (not amiodarone) (p.98).
  • St John New Zealand doses atropine at 0.6 mg (vs the US-standard 0.5 mg) with a 1.8 mg ceiling — roughly half the 3 mg US max (p.50).
  • Massachusetts is the only system to restrict targeted temperature management by scope of practice (paramedic, adults only) (p.80). Bexar/Frio uses ketamine for post-ROSC consciousness management as a standing order (p.120).
  • Lightning-strike reverse triage. The NASEMSO national model directs resuscitating cardiac-arrest victims first over the walking wounded, and states fixed/dilated pupils are not a death criterion in lightning/electrical injury (p.352) — inverting standard MCI and death-marker teaching.
  • Aortic dissection, aggressively managed. Alabama targets HR <60 and SBP <120, mandates blood pressure in both arms with treatment driven by the higher reading, and names nicardipine as the preferred vasodilator over nitroprusside (esmolol 500 mcg/kg load, then 50 mcg/kg/min, p.181).
  • BP-stratified nitroglycerin for CHF. LA County tiers SL nitro into three brackets — 0.8 mg for SBP >160, 0.4 mg for SBP 120–160, and a single 0.4 mg only for SBP 90–120 (p.56). San Diego doubles its dose by pressure (0.4 mg SL if SBP 100–150, 0.8 mg if ≥150) and bars it within 48 h of erectile-dysfunction or pulmonary-HTN drugs (p.76).

Procedures & skills

There are 1,393 procedures across the 53. The clearest pattern is that advanced procedures cluster in a handful of systems rather than appearing everywhere. Standouts:

  • Field perimortem cesarean section. Mayo Clinic Ambulance authorizes it as a Critical Care procedure, triggered at 4 minutes without ROSC in maternal cardiac arrest at ≥20 weeks gestation (p.255) — OR-level surgery in the field.
  • Field amputation in exactly one service. Massachusetts STP carries "USAR Field Amputation" (p.143), OLMC-gated, paramedic level.
  • Double Sequential External Defibrillation (DSED) is emerging in only four services. Berrien County (MI) names a hard go-live date of 10/1/25 (p.138); Aurora South WI gates it behind OLMC (p.199); Metro Regional and New Jersey also carry it.
  • Whole-blood transfusion is no longer rare (roughly a third of these systems carry the capability), but who's allowed to do it varies sharply: Denver Metro requires a Critical Care endorsement or waiver, Palm Beach Gardens names the LifeFlow rapid infuser (p.165), and Salem Health / Polk County allows blood-product transfer at the EMT-Intermediate level (p.127).
  • Finger/simple thoracostomy is rare — six services. Lake Cities buries it in a TEMS-only credentialed skill set (p.135); West Palm Beach and San Antonio specify bilateral finger thoracostomy for traumatic arrest.
  • Escharotomy reveals a scope gradient. Mayo allows it as a Critical Care standing order (p.293); AirCare/Iowa allows it OLMC-gated (p.107); Riverside REMSA lists it "monitoring only" — not authorized as a field procedure.
  • Hospital-tier POCUS on a ground service. Thurston County Medic One authorizes POCUS-guided pericardiocentesis, central venous access (IJ and femoral), and subclavian line placement, plus 7 distinct POCUS protocols (p.211).
  • Mayo authorizes prehospital fetal heart-rate monitoring via ultrasound (phased-array probe, OB/GYN mode, M-mode quantification, p.337) — extraordinarily rare for EMS.
  • iGel as first-line definitive airway. Mountain West Ambulance makes the iGel first-line citing the PART/AIRWAYS-2 trials, allowing an ETT only after 20 minutes without ROSC or iGel failure, and recommends an empiric 200 J shock for apparent asystole (p.126).
  • The TEMS standout. Lake Cities Fire (TX) carries a tactical-credentialed skill block — regional anesthesia, dental block, incision & drainage of abscess, dislocation reduction, minor wound repair, and truncal/junctional tourniquet (p.132–140). No other surveyed service field-authorizes I&D, dental blocks, or wound repair.
  • Field blood with an explicit pediatric floor. Alabama authorizes Critical Care field transfusion (1:1 PRBC:FFP or whole blood), explicitly contraindicated under age 3 or 15 kg, with a peds dose of 10 mL/kg PRBCs + 10 mL/kg FFP and required blood warmer and filtered tubing (p.208). The specific age/weight exclusion is rarely codified this plainly.
  • Surgical cric as its own subspecialty. Alabama restricts surgical cricothyrotomy to a "Tactical Paramedic Only" designation (vertical 1.5–2.5 cm incision) — separate from and above standard Critical Care — while needle cric is the standard CC backup and is preferred over surgical in pediatrics (p.207).
  • Needle thoracostomy moves to the axilla. San Diego's preferred site is the 4th/5th-ICS anterior axillary line (the TCCC/military location) rather than the traditional 2nd-ICS midclavicular line (p.16).
  • Two banned techniques. Santa Cruz expressly prohibits oxygen-powered ventilation devices and nasotracheal intubation outright, and contraindicates ETI in peds ≤12 yr or <40 kg (BLS airway only), with a max of three 15-second attempts (p.286).

Scope of practice

Scope is where the same intervention lands at very different certification levels. The clearest example: even CPAP, a near-universal skill, is EMT/BLS-level in ~20 systems but Paramedic-only in Ada County, Central Arizona, Riverside REMSA, Kentucky, Metro Regional, and Thurston County — and AEMT in PA, WV, Mountain West, Greater Miami Valley, and the NASEMSO model. Standouts:

  • New York BLS authorizes EMTs to give oral moxifloxacin 400 mg (a fluoroquinolone) for amputations/open fractures with delayed extrication (p.86/p.93) — oral antibiotics are virtually never in BLS scope anywhere else.
  • NYC REMAC authorizes EMTs to administer influenza and COVID-19 vaccines under Medical Director authorization (p.66) — unique in the set, tied to NYC's mass-vaccination program.
  • Massachusetts pushes drugs down to EMT level that are usually paramedic-or-above: buprenorphine 16 mg SL for opioid withdrawal (p.109) and oral atypical antipsychotics (risperidone, olanzapine, p.107).
  • Low-level field reduction in New York. Patella dislocation reduction and avulsed-tooth reimplantation are authorized at all levels including CFR in the NY State Collaborative (p.153/p.143) and at EMT in NY BLS — most systems restrict any reduction to ALS or physician.
  • West Virginia authorizes accessing indwelling central PICC lines in the field at AEMT and Paramedic level (GL015, p.188) — a hospital-nursing task almost never permitted in ground EMS.
  • Davidson County (NC) has an unusually deep critical-care scope at plain Paramedic level: Swan-Ganz, ventricular (IVC) drain, arterial line, and femoral central line maintenance, plus EJ IV cannulation at AEMT and LUCAS at all four levels including EMR.
  • Alberta MFR tops out at EMT with no paramedic level — King LT/LMA is the highest airway (no ETI at any level, p.7), there is no benzodiazepine for seizures anywhere in the formulary (p.33), and intranasal naloxone 4 mg goes down to Standard First Aid / FMR / EMR (p.37).
  • Sub-paramedic super-credentials gate RSI. Denver Metro requires a Critical Care endorsement or waivered paramedic (p.181), Boulder requires P-CC (p.199), San Antonio/STRAC requires a special credential plus a mandatory 2-clinician time-out (p.161), and Kentucky restricts RSI to CCTP and flight paramedic/CFRN only (p.223).
  • New York's 5-tier cert system inserts CC (Critical Care Technician) between AEMT and Paramedic, with color-coded per-level "stop bars" (CFR red / EMT orange / AEMT yellow / CC green / Paramedic blue).
  • Wisconsin EMTs cannot obtain any vascular access (a hard state scope floor, noted in the Mayo protocol p.301), while New Jersey uniquely recognizes the MICN (Mobile Intensive Care Nurse) as a full ALS provider, dual-labeling every protocol "Paramedic & MICN."
  • Alabama keeps RSI off the paramedic's hands. RSI is restricted to Critical Care level only, not Paramedic — one of the most restrictive RSI access policies in the country, where most states allow paramedic RSI (induction: ketamine 2 mg/kg IBW or etomidate 0.3 mg/kg; paralytic succinylcholine 2 mg/kg, rocuronium 1 mg/kg, or vecuronium 0.1 mg/kg, p.189).
  • Color-coded cert ladders. Alabama runs five distinct color-coded levels — EMT (white), AEMT (yellow), Intermediate (green), Paramedic (red), Critical Care (blue) — retaining the Intermediate tier and a separate Critical Care tier, a more granular ladder than the typical 3–4-level state (p.1). It echoes New York's 5-tier "stop bar" system noted above.
  • LA County's medical-control-centric scope. Even routine ALS care leans on the base hospital: push-dose epi, stable wide-complex tachycardia, magnesium, repeat midazolam, and all TOR are base-gated, and a dystonic reaction requires base contact to confirm the diagnosis before diphenhydramine is given (p.104) — rather than the usual standing-order Benadryl.
  • EMTs who draw and inject epi. Santa Cruz EMTs operating under Optional Scope of Practice (Policy 208) may draw up and inject IM epinephrine per ALS procedures — not just fire an auto-injector — for anaphylaxis and respiratory distress (p.222).

Standing-order autonomy vs OLMC gating, gate density, and certification-tier depth feed directly into the progressive-scope composite in the section below.

Where the most progressive scopes are

"Progressive scope" here is a 0–30 composite of three equally weighted sub-scores (each 0–10): (a) standing-order autonomy — how much a clinician can do without an online-medical-control call; (b) advanced skills pushed to lower certification levels — e.g. CPAP, IO, or tourniquets authorized at EMT/EMR; and (c) cutting-edge interventions — TXA, ketamine, whole blood, POCUS, field antibiotics, RSI, finger thoracostomy, ECMO routing, and similar. The three sub-scores are shown for transparency in the ranking below. It is one defensible lens, not a quality or outcome measure.

The map and ranking score ground / 911 systems only. Air-medical and critical-care-transport (CCT) services are shown separately: their broad scope reflects mission and patient mix, not jurisdictional progressiveness, so putting them on the same scale as a county 911 agency would be misleading. Statewide model documents are also held separate. Each state on the map is shaded by the highest-scoring ground system in that state — with two exceptions noted below the map (Alabama and Vermont), where the only document uploaded for that state is a statewide model.

System policies

Beyond clinical care, the protocols differ sharply in operational and medico-legal policy — destination rules, resuscitation gates, controlled-drug governance, and community programs. Standouts:

  • Riverside County (REMSA) outright bans ambulance diversion. "Ambulance diversion due to ED saturation is not permitted." A STEMI or stroke center that attempts to divert has its specialty-center designation suspended pending re-evaluation (p.340/p.351/p.363) — among the strictest anti-diversion enforcement in the set.
  • Per-administration ketamine governance. Pennsylvania audits every single ketamine administration with quarterly state reporting (p.139); Colorado (Boulder/Denver Metro) requires a state protocol waiver and mandatory CDPHE reporting for every use (Boulder p.174).
  • Family-discretion withholding of resuscitation with no written DNR/POLST. Alameda County permits withholding or ceasing resuscitation when an immediate family member or spouse unanimously requests it (p.92) — unusually permissive verbal-family authority.
  • An inverted TOR rule. Alameda County requires a maximum of "≤2 defibrillations delivered" as a termination criterion — a patient who received 3+ shocks is ineligible for field termination (p.92).
  • Anti-premature-TOR safeguard. Massachusetts states that a POCUS finding of no cardiac activity is not sufficient for TOR, and that asystole is a treatable condition (p.105) — the opposite of services that use cardiac standstill to justify termination.
  • Mandatory transport after any controlled substance. Thompson Valley requires transport for any patient who received an opioid, benzodiazepine, ketamine, or etomidate; refusal requires direct physician contact (p.489).
  • Mandatory organ-procurement notification on every field death (newborn to age 100) in Davidson County, NC, with EMS explicitly barred from discussing donation with family — rare to mandate on every prehospital pronouncement.
  • Co-responder mental-health programs. Riverside's CATT pairs EMTs with behavioral-health clinicians and transports psych patients to non-ED destinations (Policy 5802); HART pairs EMTs with community health workers for high-frequency-caller follow-up (Policy 5803).
  • Spine-board ban. Davidson County prohibits rigid spine boards during transport (permitted only for extrication), stating they do not improve outcomes and cause harm; Oklahoma excludes penetrating head/neck/torso trauma from spinal motion restriction entirely (p.284).
  • An explicit upper age cutoff for sedation. Pennsylvania restricts standing-order chemical sedation to ages 16–65 only; both <16 and >65 require OLMC (p.165).
  • CHEMPACK expiration exemption. NY State BLS states CHEMPACK meds (atropine, pralidoxime, diazepam/midazolam) may be used regardless of expiration date (p.71), and authorizes EMT-level benzodiazepines only in that scenario.
  • EMS codified as a mandatory reporter. Denver Metro names EMS as independent mandatory reporters under Colorado statute C.R.S. 19-3-304, stating that informing the receiving facility does not discharge the obligation (p.55).
  • One number for both STEMI and trauma. Alabama routes both STEMI activation and major-trauma triage through a single statewide line — the Alabama Trauma Communication Center (ATCC), 1-800-359-0123 — unifying cardiac and trauma destination decisions most states handle through separate systems (p.30).
  • Pediatric defined as ≤15, printed in green. Alabama defines pediatric explicitly as age 15 and younger (not 16 or 18) and prints every pediatric dose in green font across the 208-page book for rapid field identification (p.22).
  • Mandatory hyperbaric routing for divers. San Diego sends all diving/decompression emergencies to UCSD Hillcrest (hyperbaric capable) regardless of military status, overriding the usual military-facility preference, and instructs crews to bring the patient's dive computer and gear (p.65).
  • A county with no trauma centers. Santa Cruz has zero trauma centers in the county and triages all major trauma out-of-county using a proprietary three-tier PAM tool (Physiologic / Anatomic / Mechanistic, p.239). Its naloxone 4 mg IN is a standing BLS order paired with a Leave-Behind Naloxone program (Policy 634) that lets crews hand naloxone directly to bystanders at overdose scenes (p.220).
  • A public-health phone tree for drownings. LA County mandates Department of Public Health notification (213-989-7140) for every pool/spa drowning — a county-specific reporting requirement built into the clinical protocol (p.84).

Outliers & notable quirks

Some items do not fit a clean category — either because they are singular capabilities or because they are visible document-assembly artifacts.

  • Prehospital STEMI fibrinolysis. St John New Zealand carries tenecteplase (weight/age-based IV) plus heparin 5000 U as a standing order for STEMI, with a STEMI coordinator (not generic OLMC) as the decision contact (p.62). No US system in the set gives field thrombolytics for STEMI. St John NZ also uses a fixed non-escalating 200 J for all adult defibrillation, aligning with ANZCOR rather than AHA/ERC.
  • Double EpiPen. Greater Miami Valley uniquely instructs giving both an adult EpiPen 0.3 mg and an EpiPen Jr 0.15 mg simultaneously (0.45 mg total) for anaphylaxis patients ≥30 kg when using auto-injectors. It also runs a formal JITSO ("Just In Time Standing Order") system where backup drugs are physically bagged only when the primary is unavailable.
  • Transvenous pacing in one service. Boulder County is the only system carrying maintenance/monitoring of transvenous pacing, restricted to P-CC and IFT-only (p.199). Transcutaneous pacing, by contrast, is near-universal (36 services).
  • Self-administered olanzapine. Alameda County hands an olanzapine ODT tablet to a cooperative psychiatric patient to self-administer sublingually (EMS does not administer it, p.133).
  • An ICU formulary aloft. UT LIFESTAR carries buccal misoprostol 400 mcg for postpartum hemorrhage plus clevidipine, milrinone, nitroprusside, propofol, dexmedetomidine, and IV potassium infusions (p.224).
  • Michigan's Special Pathogen Response Network (Sections 10-10 to 10-18) includes a negative-pressure vinyl Medical Isolation Transport Device, ambulance decon procedures, and a "Death During Transport" protocol (no resuscitation; contact CHECC for crematorium destination) — developed from the Ebola response, far beyond the single-paragraph infectious-disease guidance in most state sets (p.410).
  • Alberta's error-trap specificity. The Alberta MFR protocol prints an explicit warning that 1:1000 epinephrine should never be given IV (a common prehospital error) and uses an age-based pediatric hypotension formula, 70 + (2 × age) mmHg, as the anaphylaxis fluid-bolus trigger (p.19–20).
  • A doctor's cell phone in the protocol. Lake Cities prints the EMS Medical Director's personal cell number, and its TEMS abscess I&D protocol routes a texted photo to a named physician for an antibiotic prescription (p.116).
  • A documented state-driven reversal. A Colorado mandate stripped ketamine-for-agitation from Thompson Valley EMS (removed June 2021) and deleted the term "Excited Delirium" entirely on 5/27/24.
  • Visible draft artifacts. The NASEMSO national model's published PDF contains an editorial comment in the prednisone appendix: "WILL NEED TO REVIEW EVERY PLACE PREDNISONE IS MENTIONED IN THE PROTOCOLS" (p.390). Kershaw County has two protocols sharing the number 7.14 and embeds a 2017 state dialysis protocol verbatim inside a 2025 document.
  • A dangling drug reference. West Virginia's midazolam entry lists it as "an adjunct to Haloperidol," but haloperidol has no formulary entry and is not stocked anywhere in the protocol (p.230).
  • An internal concentration discrepancy. Oklahoma's phenylephrine header reads "2% (NEOSYNEPHRINE)" while its How-Supplied section states a 1% solution (p.27) — a discrepancy that could cause dosing confusion.
  • No vinegar on the Pacific coast. LA County explicitly prohibits vinegar for jellyfish stings in California waters, noting local Pacific species are not vinegar-responsive nematocyst types and that vinegar may worsen the reaction (p.76) — a region-specific reversal of the standard tropical-jellyfish advice.

How we did this

These 53 protocols are exactly the ones that real ProtoQuiz users uploaded to study — we did not go looking for them, providers brought them. Each PDF was read page by page by an AI model, in roughly 20-page chunks, pulling out the same set of fields every time: which service it is, its treatment algorithms, its medications (with dose, route, and page), its procedures, and anything unusual. Every fact in this post is tied to the page it came from. The reading ran quietly on a Raspberry Pi over several days. Every number here is counted across those same 53 protocols.

Every standout in this survey was then independently fact-checked by three separate AI skeptic agents, each instructed to re-open the cited file and page and default to refuting the claim unless the source plainly supported it. An item appears here only if at least two of the three confirmed it.

Why this matters for studying

The breadth is the point. Across these 53 systems there is no single right answer for most of prehospital care — not the adenosine start, not the anaphylaxis epi dose, not who is allowed to perform CPAP or RSI, not whether your service can divert or carry whole blood, not even whether maple syrup counts as oral glucose. Generic NREMT material teaches the conventional version; your agency's protocol is the one that holds up on a call.
